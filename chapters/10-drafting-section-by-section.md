# Chapter 10 — Drafting (Section by Section)
*Draft in the order that protects honesty; arrange in the order readers expect.*

A student writes the Introduction first. This is the natural impulse — the Introduction is the beginning, so you begin there. The study isn't done yet, but the framing can be established, the stakes can be laid out, the hypothesis can be introduced with appropriate authority.

Weeks later, the data are collected and analyzed. The findings are real but narrower than the hypothesis promised. The study was designed to show that AI tutoring causes long-term learning gains; it found an association between tutoring frequency and immediate quiz scores. The Introduction is beautifully written. It promises a causal study of durable learning. And it is now wrong — not because the science failed, but because the framing was written before the findings were known, in a form that the findings cannot support.

Rewriting the Introduction to match the actual results takes more than editing. It takes dismantling an argument that was constructed to support a different paper, and rebuilding it to support this one. The labor is real. More importantly, the mistake was avoidable. The Introduction was written first not because it needs to be written first — it doesn't — but because that is where it appears in the final document. Confusing reading order with drafting order is a surprisingly easy mistake to make, and a costly one.

---

The empirical paper has a conventional reading order: Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion, Abstract. That order was designed for readers, not writers. It presents the context before the findings, which is what a reader needs to understand what they're about to encounter. But a writer who drafts in reading order is constantly working with information they don't yet have: writing an Introduction before knowing what the results are, writing a Discussion before knowing what the Results section will say.

The drafting order that protects honesty runs almost in reverse. It starts with what you actually did and found, and works outward from there.

**Methods first.** Methods report what was done — the design, participants, materials, procedure, and analysis plan. This section can be drafted from the protocol and the actual record of what happened, without waiting for results. It is the most purely procedural section of the paper. Its job is replicability: a reader who follows the Methods description should be able to run the same study. If you cannot describe the procedure clearly enough for that, the description is incomplete, not the study.

Reporting standards exist precisely because readers cannot evaluate findings without design details. JARS specifies what a psychology study's methods must report. CONSORT does the same for clinical trials. STROBE for observational epidemiology. The details vary, but the underlying logic is consistent: the reader's ability to judge your results depends entirely on knowing how those results were produced. A Methods section that omits how outliers were handled, how randomization was implemented, or what the pre-specified primary outcome was is a Methods section that prevents evaluation.

**Results next.** Results report what was found. Not what it means. Not why it happened. Not what it implies for practice or theory. What happened, expressed in the strongest, most precise terms the evidence supports, organized in hypothesis order so the paper's central question gets answered before secondary analyses are reported.

The most common violation of Results section discipline is interpretation. "The Socratic feedback group performed significantly better, suggesting that retrieval effort is the operative mechanism." The first clause belongs in Results. The second clause belongs in Discussion. The Results section can say what was found, can report effect sizes and confidence intervals and whether the primary hypothesis was supported, but cannot explain why. Explanation requires inference beyond the data. Inference is the Discussion's job.

This separation is not bureaucratic formalism. It is epistemically important. Results that contain interpretation are harder to evaluate, because the interpretation is mixed with the finding and readers must disentangle them. A clean Results section allows a reader to receive the finding on its own terms and form their own interpretation before encountering yours.

**Discussion after.** The Discussion interprets the Results: what do the findings mean, what do they not mean, how do they fit with prior work, what are the limitations, what are the implications. This is where the inferential work happens — where you move from "here is what was found" to "here is what it means and what it doesn't."

The Discussion is also where honest limits live. A finding from an underpowered study, or a finding with wide confidence intervals, or a finding that was the third of seven comparisons — the Discussion is where those qualifications belong. Not as apologies, but as honest accounting of what the evidence can and cannot support.

A Discussion that only elaborates the positive findings and buries the limitations in a final paragraph is not doing its job. The limits shape the claim. A claim without its limits is a different claim — a stronger one than the evidence supports.

---

**Introduction fourth.** The Introduction is the last substantive section to draft, not the first. It is written after you know what the paper actually found, because the Introduction frames the paper that exists, not the paper you hoped to write.

The Introduction does three things (CARS from Chapter 9): establishes the territory, establishes the niche, and occupies the niche with the present study. When the Introduction is drafted last, the niche can be drawn precisely to fit the actual contribution — not a broad gesture toward a field that any study could fill, but a specific gap whose specific shape matches the specific design and results of this paper.

The student whose Introduction promised a causal study of long-term learning made an error that is common among writers who draft in reading order: they committed to a frame before they knew what the evidence would support. Drafting the Introduction last prevents this. The frame is built to fit the findings, rather than the findings being squeezed into a pre-existing frame.

**Abstract fifth.** The Abstract is a compression of the complete paper. It cannot be written until the complete paper exists, because it must accurately report what Methods, Results, and Discussion actually say — not what you intended them to say before you wrote them.

A good abstract stands alone. A reader who encounters the abstract without access to the full paper should understand: what was studied, how, in what population, with what findings, with what limitations, and with what implications. The abstract is not a teaser. It is a miniature version of the argument. A reader who reads only the abstract and draws a conclusion from it should draw the same conclusion they would draw from reading the full paper.

The most common abstract failure is misalignment with the Results section: the abstract claims a stronger finding than the Results report, or claims certainty about a mechanism the Discussion carefully hedges. This happens when the abstract is written from memory or from notes rather than from the finished paper. The remedy is to write the abstract last, and to check it line-by-line against the sections it compresses.

**Title last.** The title is a search tool and a promise. It is a search tool because researchers find papers through keywords, and the title is the most weighted location for keywords in most search systems. It is a promise because the title names what the paper delivers, and a reader who follows up on a title that doesn't match the paper's content has been misled.

A good title names the specific contribution with enough context to be useful to a searching reader: the intervention, the outcome, the population, and ideally the key finding or frame. "AI Tutoring and Student Learning" is a title about a topic. "Socratic AI Feedback Improves Two-Week Retention in Undergraduate Programming: A Randomized Study" is a title about a specific claim. The second title tells a searching researcher whether this paper is relevant to their question without requiring them to open it.

---

There is a tool that makes the drafting order easier to execute, and it is simple enough that it is easy to underestimate: the key-message sentence.

Before drafting any section, write one sentence that captures what that section must establish. Not what it will contain — what it must accomplish. The key-message sentence for the Results section might be: "Socratic feedback produced higher delayed retention scores with a medium effect size, and the confidence interval excludes zero, but the direct-answer group's individual scores overlapped substantially." That sentence tells you what the Results section needs to show, and it tells you what the section should not do — it should not claim certainty about the mechanism, should not report only the group means without the overlap, should not bury the confidence interval.

The key-message sentence is not part of the section. It is a constraint on the section. When you have drafted the section, check it against the key-message sentence: does the section establish what it needed to establish? If the section says less than the key-message sentence, something is missing. If the section says more — if it makes claims the key-message sentence doesn't license — the section has drifted.

The key-message sentences for all six sections should form a coherent argument when read in sequence. If the Methods key message describes a two-condition experiment with a delayed test, the Results key message should report what the delayed test found, the Discussion key message should interpret that finding with its limits, the Introduction key message should establish the gap that makes the delayed test important, the Abstract key message should compress all five into a standalone summary, and the Title should name the finding precisely enough to be retrieved by the right reader.

If those six sentences contradict each other, or if one section promises what another can't deliver, the paper has a structural problem that prose quality cannot fix.

---

The chapter's principle has a corollary that is worth making explicit: this drafting order is for empirical papers with known results. It assumes you have data and findings before you write. When you don't — when a paper begins with a theoretical argument that the empirical work will test, or when you're writing a conceptual review — the logic of drafting order changes, though the principle doesn't. Don't frame more than the paper can support. The drafting order is a means to that end, not the end itself.

Humanities papers, legal scholarship, theoretical modeling work — these have different section structures and different relationships between argument and evidence. The Methods-first advice doesn't apply in the same way. But the discipline of asking, for each section, "what is this section's specific job, and am I doing that job and not another section's job" — that discipline applies everywhere.

The common thread is protection against a specific kind of failure: writing a frame before you know what it needs to frame, and then being unable to update the frame honestly because you are attached to it. The introduction a student writes on day one of a project is almost always wrong by the time the project is done — not wrong about the topic, but wrong about the specific contribution, because that contribution isn't fully visible until the work is finished. The drafting order is a structural guarantee that the frame gets written last, when the contribution is known.

## Exercises

### Warm-up

**1.** For a paper you are writing or planning to write, write six key-message sentences — one for each section in drafting order: Methods, Results, Discussion, Introduction, Abstract, Title. Read all six in sequence. Do they form a coherent argument? Does any section promise more than the previous sections license? Note where the chain breaks and what would need to change.

**2.** Find a Results section in a published paper. Read it once and mark every sentence that contains interpretation — any sentence that explains why findings occurred, what they imply, or what they suggest about mechanisms. Count the interpretive sentences. Then find the Discussion section and check whether the same interpretations appear there. Note: if the interpretation appears only in Results and not in Discussion, it may be the only place the reader encounters it.

### Application

**3.** Take a paragraph from a Results section you've written or found that contains interpretation. Remove every interpretive phrase and rewrite the paragraph as pure finding — what was found, with what measure, at what magnitude, with what uncertainty. Then write the interpretive content that was removed as a new Discussion paragraph. Compare the two versions: what does the separation make visible that the combined version obscured?

**4.** Write the Abstract for a study that hasn't been written yet, using only the key-message sentences from Exercise 1. The abstract should stand alone — someone who reads it without seeing the full paper should understand the design, findings, and limits. Then check it against each key-message sentence: does the abstract accurately represent what each section will establish?

### Synthesis

**5.** A paper's Introduction promises "a randomized study demonstrating that Socratic AI feedback causes durable learning gains in novice programmers." The Results section reports: "The Socratic feedback group scored higher on a two-week delayed retention test (*t*(62) = 2.47, *p* = .016, *d* = 0.63), though the effect was not significant when controlling for pre-test score (*t*(61) = 1.84, *p* = .07, *d* = 0.47)." Write the Discussion paragraph that honestly interprets this result, and then rewrite the Introduction's promise to accurately frame what the paper actually found. Explain what specific language in the original Introduction had to change, and why.

**6.** Explain why drafting the Introduction last, rather than first, protects against a specific type of intellectual dishonesty. Use the student's case from the chapter's opening — the causal promise that the associational data couldn't support — as your example. Describe the mechanism by which writing the Introduction first creates the problem, and the mechanism by which writing it last prevents it.

### Challenge

**7.** Take a published paper whose Introduction you believe overpromises relative to its findings. Identify the specific mismatch: what does the Introduction claim, and what do the Results actually show? Write a revised Introduction that accurately frames the paper's actual contribution without underplaying it. Then write the key-message sentence the original Introduction should have been built around, and explain what would have needed to change in the study's design or framing for the original Introduction to be defensible.
