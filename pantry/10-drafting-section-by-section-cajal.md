Figure type: Process flowchart (dual parallel sequences)
Concept statement: Papers are read Introduction-first but should be drafted Methods-first, because each drafting step only becomes possible once the prior step's content exists.
Reader prior knowledge: Knows the IMRaD section names; has likely never seen reading order and drafting order set side by side as deliberately opposed sequences.

Right column, the drafting sequence, holds six boxes: Methods, Results, Discussion, Introduction, Abstract, Title. Color the right-column boxes by readiness logic: Methods, Results, Discussion in primary blue #0072B2 (drafted from known content), Introduction and Abstract in secondary orange #E69F00 (depend on the blue boxes existing first), Title in neutral gray. Between the two columns draw three thin horizontal connector arrows in gray linking the same section across columns, showing how the four shared sections reorder. ## Figure 2: Each section's job

Figure type: Hierarchy/taxonomy (matrix of cells)
Concept statement: Each paper section has one specific job and one boundary it must not cross, and the violations are the places adjacent sections leak into each other.
Reader prior knowledge: Knows section names but tends to blur the Results/Discussion boundary; has not seen the jobs laid out as bounded territories.
Source anchor: Section: bullet list "Before the drafting order makes sense..." and the embedded TABLE comment

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw a vertical stack of six horizontal bands on white, one band per section, in drafting order from top: Methods, Results, Discussion, Introduction, Abstract, Title. Within each band place two adjacent cells: a left cell sized larger representing the section's job, and a right cell representing the forbidden territory it must not enter. Color the left job-cells in active green #009E73 to read as the permitted work. Color the right boundary-cells in blocking vermilion #D55E00 to read as the line not to cross. Between the Results band's forbidden cell and the Discussion band's job cell, draw one short single-headed gray arrow pointing downward, showing that interpretation belongs one band lower. Keep all bands the same width, left-aligned, evenly spaced. Flat unannotated vector, 1pt strokes, white background, no text — the two-cell split and the green/vermilion encoding carry the job-versus-boundary distinction.

## Figure 3: The key-message constraint
Priority: Important
Trigger: VG — the key-message sentence as an external constraint that catches both under- and over-delivery is a structural relationship, not a depictable object
Concept statement: The key-message sentence sits outside a section as a constraint; the section can drift by saying less than it promises or more than it licenses, and the constraint catches both failures.
Reader prior knowledge: New to the idea of a sentence that is "not part of the section" but governs it; needs the externality made visible.
Source anchor: Section: "There is a tool that makes the drafting order easier..."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw one central rounded rectangle representing a drafted section, sitting on white. Above it, offset and clearly separate, draw a smaller horizontal capsule representing the key-message constraint, in secondary orange #E69F00. From the orange capsule draw a single-headed gray arrow pointing down to the section rectangle, marking that the constraint governs the section. To the left of the section draw a downward-pointing chevron in blocking vermilion #D55E00 labeled by position as "under-delivery" (section says less than promised); to the right draw an upward-pointing chevron also in vermilion marking "over-delivery" (section claims more than licensed). Center the section box itself in active green #009E73 to read as the calibrated, aligned state. Keep the orange capsule visibly outside and above the green box to encode that the constraint is not part of the section. Flat unannotated vector, 1pt strokes, white, no text — geometry and color carry under/over/aligned.

## Figure 4: The frame-before-findings failure
Priority: Supplementary
Trigger: MC — the opening anecdote is a 4-step causal chain (frame early → findings narrower → mismatch → costly rebuild) that the reader must hold in sequence
Figure type: Process flowchart (failure chain vs. protected chain)
Concept statement: Writing the Introduction first commits to a frame before the evidence exists, forcing a costly rebuild; writing it last lets the frame fit the findings.
Reader prior knowledge: Has read the student anecdote; benefits from seeing the two timelines diverge at the commitment point.
Source anchor: Section: opening (student writes Introduction first) and "The common thread is protection..."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw two horizontal timelines stacked on white, left to right, sharing a common start node. The top timeline (the failure path) runs: frame-commitment node, then findings-arrive node, then a mismatch node, then a rebuild node — color the frame-commitment and rebuild nodes blocking vermilion #D55E00 and the mismatch node vermilion as well, with the findings node neutral gray. The bottom timeline (the protected path) runs: findings-arrive node first, then frame-commitment node, then an aligned node — color findings and frame nodes primary blue #0072B2 and the aligned end node active green #009E73. Connect nodes within each timeline by single-headed rightward gray arrows. Vertically align the findings-arrive node across both timelines with a thin dashed gray guide so the reader sees the same event placed early in one path and late in the other. Flat unannotated vector, 1pt strokes, white, no text — node order and color encode failure versus protection.
