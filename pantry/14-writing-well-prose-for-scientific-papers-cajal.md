## Figure 1: Nominalization — Action Hidden vs. Action Restored
Priority: Critical
Trigger: VG — a structural transformation (action buried in a noun vs. action restored to subject-verb-object) that the prose contrasts but does not show spatially
Figure type: Comparison panels
Concept statement: A nominalization buries the sentence's action inside a noun, collapsing the actor and verb; restoring the verb re-exposes a clear subject, verb, and object the reader can follow.
Reader prior knowledge: Readers know nouns and verbs but have not seen the structural difference between a buried-action and a restored-action sentence laid out spatially.
Source anchor: Section: "Nominalization is the most common source of fog..." through "...a structure the reader can follow."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw a blank unannotated flat vector two-panel comparison on a white background, panels stacked vertically and equal in size, separated by one thin horizontal gray rule. In the upper panel, draw a single wide rounded rectangle filled vermilion (#D55E00) representing one dense noun-block, with a small neutral gray (#808080) circle nested inside it at center to suggest the action trapped within the noun; no other shapes, signaling collapsed structure. In the lower panel, draw three separate equal-height boxes arranged left to right in a row, connected by two single-headed straight black arrows pointing rightward: the leftmost box filled sky blue (#56B4E9) for the subject, the middle box filled bluish green (#009E73) for the restored verb, the rightmost box filled secondary orange (#E69F00) for the object. Use 1pt black strokes on all shapes and arrows. Align the lower row on a shared horizontal baseline; keep the upper block centered. No text, labels, letters, icons, shadows, or gradients anywhere; maintain even white margins.

## Figure 2: Passive Voice — Informative vs. Evasive
Priority: Important
Trigger: VG — a structural distinction (actor irrelevant and appropriately suppressed vs. actor concealed to hide a decision) that the prose argues but does not depict
Figure type: Comparison panels
Concept statement: Informative passive suppresses an actor who is genuinely irrelevant, while evasive passive conceals a present decision-maker; the test is whether naming the actor would reveal something the sentence is hiding.
Reader prior knowledge: Readers hold the blanket rule "avoid passive"; they lack the two-category distinction this chapter draws.
Source anchor: Section: "Passive voice is the element of scientific prose..." through "...the decision should be reported."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw a blank unannotated flat vector two-panel comparison on a white background, panels side by side and equal in width, separated by one thin vertical gray rule. In the left panel, draw a central process box filled sky blue (#56B4E9) and, to its left, an empty dashed-outline placeholder box (1pt black dashed stroke, no fill) with a single light gray (#808080) thin arrow pointing from the empty placeholder to the process box, signaling an actor slot that is genuinely empty and irrelevant. In the right panel, draw an identical central process box filled vermilion (#D55E00) and, to its left, a solid-filled actor box filled secondary orange (#E69F00) that is partially hidden behind a neutral gray (#808080) opaque rectangle overlapping its right side, with a single black single-headed arrow from the hidden actor box to the process box, signaling a real decision-maker being concealed. Use 1pt black strokes throughout; single-headed arrows only; align both panels on a shared horizontal centerline. No text, labels, icons, shadows, or gradients; keep clean white margins.

## Figure 3: Epistemic Verb Calibration Scale
Priority: Critical
Trigger: VG — an ordered relationship (five verbs ranked by evidence strength they imply) that the prose lists linearly but never renders as a calibrated scale
Figure type: Conceptual map
Concept statement: Epistemic verbs form an ordered scale from strongest to weakest evidential commitment — demonstrates, shows, indicates, suggests, is consistent with — and the verb must be matched to the actual strength of the study's evidence.
Reader prior knowledge: Readers use these verbs interchangeably; the figure orders them as a graded continuum tied to evidence strength.
Source anchor: Section: "The most consequential prose choice... is the epistemic verb" through "...change the verb."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw a blank unannotated flat vector horizontal calibration scale on a white background: one long horizontal axis line (1pt black) running left to right with five evenly spaced tick segments, each segment marked by a vertical box sitting on the axis. From left (strongest) to right (weakest), draw five equal-sized rectangles graded in fill: box one solid blue (#0072B2), box two sky blue (#56B4E9), box three secondary orange (#E69F00), box four lighter neutral gray (#808080), box five a lighter gray outline box with white fill and 1pt black stroke. Below the axis, draw a single thin gray (#808080) horizontal wedge or tapering triangle that is tallest under the leftmost box and narrows to a point under the rightmost box, encoding decreasing evidence strength left to right. Keep all five boxes equal in size and evenly spaced on the axis. Use 1pt black strokes throughout. No text, numbers, verb labels, icons, shadows, or gradients; maintain even white margins and clean alignment.

## Figure 4: Gopen-Swan Sentence Positions
Priority: Important
Trigger: VG — a structural reading model (topic position at the start, stress position at the end) that the prose describes but does not lay out as sentence geometry
Figure type: Structural schematic
Concept statement: Readers expect familiar context in the opening topic position and new important information in the closing stress position; a sentence that inverts these — burying the finding at the front and ending on a reference number — misdirects attention.
Reader prior knowledge: Readers write sentences without a model of where readers expect old vs. new information.
Source anchor: Section: "The sentence structure principle... comes from... Gopen and Swan" through "...Information in the right positions."

### Block 1 — Illustrae Paste Block
Draw a blank unannotated flat vector two-row schematic on a white background, two equal-width horizontal bars stacked vertically, each bar representing one sentence and divided into three contiguous segments left to right. The top bar is the correct sentence: leftmost segment filled sky blue (#56B4E9) for the topic (familiar) position, a wide middle segment filled white with 1pt black outline for the body, and a rightmost segment filled bluish green (#009E73) for the stress (new information) position. The bottom bar is the inverted sentence: leftmost segment filled bluish green (#009E73) showing new information misplaced at the front, white outlined middle body, and a rightmost segment filled neutral gray (#808080) showing a trivial element (a reference number slot) landing in the stress position. Add one small thin black caret or bracket above the rightmost segment of the top bar and above the rightmost segment of the bottom bar to flag the stress position in both. Use 1pt black strokes, equal bar heights, shared left alignment. No text, numbers, letters, icons, shadows, or gradients; keep even white margins.
