## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Smith, _Peer review: a flawed process at the heart of science and journals_ (2006, Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine).** A critical overview useful for demystifying peer review without romanticizing it.
- **Tennant et al., _A multi-disciplinary perspective on emergent and future innovations in peer review_ (2017, F1000Research).** Surveys open, post-publication, portable, and collaborative peer-review models.
- **Munafo et al., _A manifesto for reproducible science_ (2017, Nature Human Behaviour).** Frames peer review as one part of a broader credibility ecosystem, not the only safeguard.
- **JAMA / ICMJE AI guidance, _Guidance for authors, reviewers, and editors on AI use_ (2023 onward, editorial guidance).** Important for simulating review ethically: confidentiality and AI-use policies constrain uploading manuscripts to tools.

### Key empirical cases
- **Structured reviewer form simulation.** Critical/Major/Minor review with section references mirrors real revision triage.
- **Venue-specific review contrast.** Same paper reviewed as CHI vs medical journal vs education journal to reveal community norms.
- **Cross-section consistency review.** Compare Abstract to Results and Introduction hypotheses to Results tests.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Peer review evaluates contribution, method, evidence, writing, fit, and ethics. Simulated review is useful when it is specific, section-grounded, and critical rather than generic encouragement.

### What is disputed
Peer review has known weaknesses: bias, inconsistency, conservatism, and confidentiality concerns. AI-assisted simulation can help authors prepare but cannot replace human domain review.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
Reviewer use of AI is restricted by many journals because of confidentiality and accountability. Authors should not upload confidential manuscripts where policies prohibit it.

- Translate review comments into revision tasks.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This chapter tests whether the schema is visible to an outside evaluator. The student must decide which simulated criticisms are valid; AI can generate adversarial checks but may invent standards or miss field norms.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Novices read review as personal judgment. Teach classification: fatal flaw, major repair, minor polish, out-of-scope preference.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to simulate a rigorous reviewer with Critical, Major, and Minor issues.
- Use AI for narrow cross-checks: Abstract versus Results, Introduction hypotheses versus Results tests, Discussion claims versus design.
- Use AI to generate a revision table from reviewer-style comments.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not upload confidential manuscripts, unpublished participant data, or proprietary material to tools forbidden by journal, funder, institution, or collaborator policy.
- Do not treat AI review as a substitute for human domain review.
- Do not accept all AI reviewer comments; some will be generic, false, or misaligned with the venue.

### Disclosure and accountability note
AI-assisted peer-review simulation may require disclosure depending on venue and confidentiality rules. Keep prompts and outputs if the journal asks for process records.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Act as a rigorous reviewer for [venue]. Give Critical, Major, and Minor issues with section references, then name the single strongest reason to reject the paper.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
