## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **IMRaD historical and scientific-writing literature, _IMRaD structure for scientific papers_ (20th century onward, genre convention).** The chapter rests on IMRaD as a stabilized reporting genre; use sources on scientific article structure to avoid treating the order as merely personal advice.
- **Swales and Feak, _Academic Writing for Graduate Students_ (multiple editions, textbook).** Supports section-specific writing moves, especially introductions and abstracts.
- **Appelbaum et al., _Journal Article Reporting Standards for Quantitative Research in Psychology_ (2018, American Psychologist).** Useful for Methods and Results content requirements.
- **CONSORT and STROBE statements, _Reporting guidelines for trials and observational studies_ (2007/2010, guidelines).** Provide concrete section-level reporting expectations.

### Key empirical cases
- **Methods replication test.** Give a Methods section to a peer and collect missing procedural questions.
- **Results interpretation removal.** Highlight every phrase in Results that explains why, then move it to Discussion.
- **Five-sentence abstract compression.** Problem, gap, method, finding, implication as a drafting exercise.

---

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Research article sections have different jobs. Methods report procedure, Results report findings, Discussion interprets, Introduction frames, Abstract compresses, and Title signals the claim/scope.

### What is disputed
The best drafting order varies by writer and discipline. The book's Methods -> Results -> Discussion -> Introduction -> Abstract -> Title order is pedagogically defensible for empirical papers, but not universal for theoretical or humanities work.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
AI tools are increasingly useful for code, formatting, and compression, but journal policies and authorship accountability constrain how generated text can be used.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

- Draft Methods from the protocol before polishing prose.
- Report Results in hypothesis order.
- Write Discussion around meaning and limits.
- Write Introduction after knowing what the paper actually delivered.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This chapter operationalizes labor separation: thinking and evidence first, prose second. The student must supply the section's key message; AI can tighten or format after the content exists.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Students often write in reading order, causing promises that no longer match results. Teach with section job cards and cross-section alignment checks.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 9. Sourcing Notes

Primary sources are strongest for methodology, statistics, causal inference, visualization, and genre theory. ## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to check whether a Methods section is replicable, whether Results contain interpretation, and whether Discussion answers the stated research question.
- Use AI to compress a human-written five-sentence abstract into a journal word limit.
- Use AI to format citations or improve transitions after the section key message is fixed.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not ask AI to draft sections before the claim, evidence, and key message are settled.
- Do not let AI write an Introduction that promises a stronger claim than the Results deliver.
- Do not accept AI formatting of references without checking author order, title, year, DOI, and style.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI substantially drafted, compressed, or edited manuscript sections, disclose according to target journal policy. The author must be able to defend every sentence.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Here is my Results section. Flag every sentence that interprets, explains, or implies causation rather than reporting what the data showed.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
