## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Karl Popper, _The Logic of Scientific Discovery_ (1959, book).** Foundational for falsifiability and the asymmetry between verification and refutation. This chapter should use Popper operationally: what observation would make the assertion false?
- **Jerzy Neyman and Egon Pearson, _On the Problem of the Most Efficient Tests of Statistical Hypotheses_ (1933, Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society A).** Anchors null/alternative testing as a decision framework rather than proof. Useful for the chapter's warning that statistical rejection is not truth.
- **Paul Meehl, _Theory-testing in psychology and physics: A methodological paradox_ (1967, Philosophy of Science).** Shows why weak theories can survive by absorbing ambiguous results. Excellent support for the chapter's attack on underdefined hypotheses.
- **Kerr, _HARKing: Hypothesizing After the Results are Known_ (1998, Personality and Social Psychology Review).** Names the failure mode where post-hoc assertions are written as if they were planned.

### Key empirical cases
- **Bem precognition controversy and subsequent replication debate.** A vivid case of statistically significant findings that stress-test assertion, prior plausibility, and replication. Good for teaching skepticism without ridicule.
- **Many Labs replication projects.** Show how planned replications distinguish assertions that travel from assertions that were context-bound or fragile.
- **Illustrative assertion taxonomy exercise.** Give six claims from one student topic and classify as descriptive, causal, correlational, predictive, mechanistic, normative, or conceptual.

---

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Assertion type determines evidence type. Causal, correlational, predictive, mechanistic, normative, and conceptual claims are not interchangeable, and statistical hypothesis testing does not prove an alternative hypothesis.

### What is disputed
Philosophers and statisticians still debate falsification, Bayesian alternatives, and whether null-hypothesis significance testing should remain central. The chapter should teach NHST as a common language with known limits, not as the whole logic of science.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
Open-science reforms have made planned hypotheses, replication, and transparency more central to methods teaching. AI adds a new risk: students can generate plausible assertion taxonomies without understanding the evidential consequences.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

- Classify claims in an Introduction before designing the study.
- Rewrite causal verbs as associational language when design is correlational.
- State a concrete null and alternative hypothesis.
- List three observations that would defeat the proposed assertion.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

The book argues that papers fail when their central claim is vague; this chapter gives the taxonomy for naming the claim before writing. The student must supply the actual assertion and its defeaters; AI can audit type consistency.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Misconceptions include "p < .05 proves my hypothesis" and "a research question is the same as a hypothesis." Teach with sorting tasks, counterexamples, and sentence-level verb audits.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to classify claims as descriptive, causal, correlational, predictive, mechanistic, normative, or conceptual.
- Use AI to generate possible falsification conditions and null hypotheses for a draft claim.
- Use AI to audit a paragraph for assertion-type drift, especially causal verbs appearing inside correlational evidence.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not use AI classification as authoritative when the domain context changes the claim type.
- Do not let AI turn a normative claim into an empirical one or erase values language that should be explicit.
- Do not accept a generated null hypothesis if it does not match the actual design, measure, or comparison.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI performed assertion typing or falsifiability checks, disclose it as an audit process. The final assertion type and null/alternative statements remain the author responsibility.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Classify each claim below by assertion type. For each empirical claim, state the evidence required and one observation that would defeat it.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
