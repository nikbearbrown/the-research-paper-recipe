# Research: Chapter 01 — Before You Write Anything
## The Research Paper Recipe

**Chapter one-line:** Before You Write — Topic -> Research Question -> Working Hypothesis
**Research date:** 2026-05-29

---

## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Karl Popper, _The Logic of Scientific Discovery_ (1959 English edition; original 1934, book).** Supplies the falsifiability frame behind the chapter's warning that a topic is not yet a claim. Use it to make the "claim that can fail" standard philosophically grounded without turning the chapter into philosophy of science.
- **Robert K. Merton, _The Normative Structure of Science_ (1942, essay/chapter).** Gives the chapter a vocabulary for research as public knowledge work, not private prose production. It supports the book's emphasis on organized skepticism and communal scrutiny before polished writing.
- **Nosek, Ebersole, DeHaven, and Mellor, _The preregistration revolution_ (2018, PNAS).** Useful for distinguishing exploratory hunches from confirmatory hypotheses. It gives a practical modern mechanism for turning an initial idea into a declared, testable plan.
- **Simmons, Nelson, and Simonsohn, _False-Positive Psychology_ (2011, Psychological Science).** Shows why flexible thinking after data exposure makes plausible stories too easy. This supports the chapter's insistence that hypothesis formation precede drafting and analysis.

### Key empirical cases
- **Open Science Framework preregistration templates.** Researchers convert broad ideas into hypotheses, variables, sampling plans, and analysis decisions before data collection. The case is appropriate because novice writers can see the difference between a topic and a registered claim.
- **Registered Reports at journals such as Cortex and Royal Society Open Science.** Peer review happens before results are known, forcing the research question and design to do the intellectual work. This works as an opening contrast to traditional writing-first student papers.
- **Illustrative classroom case: AI tutoring in undergraduate programming.** A student begins with "AI tutoring" as a topic, narrows to feedback specificity, then states a delayed-retention hypothesis. Label as illustrative unless tied to a real dataset.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
A research paper needs a specific, answerable question and a claim that evidence could disconfirm. The field is broadly aligned that undisclosed flexibility in hypothesis formation weakens evidentiary value, and preregistration/registered reports make the timing of claims visible.

### What is disputed
The debated edge is how rigid early hypothesis commitment should be. Exploratory work is legitimate, but it must be labeled as exploratory; the author should avoid implying that all valuable research begins with a fully formed confirmatory hypothesis.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
The last five years have made AI-assisted ideation common, which raises the risk that students outsource the hypothesis itself. Current journal norms increasingly ask authors to disclose substantive AI assistance, so AI use in early reasoning is a live policy issue.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

- Turn a broad assignment prompt into a researchable question using population, variable, comparison, and outcome.
- Convert an observation from a course project into three rival hypotheses and choose the one the student can actually test.
- Draft a pre-analysis skeleton before opening a manuscript file.
- Use AI only to pressure-test testability, not to decide the student's intellectual claim.

---

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This chapter establishes the book's central claim: prose should wait until the schema exists. The student must supply judgment about what matters, what can be tested, and what the evidence could defeat; AI can interrogate that judgment but cannot own it.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 5. The AI Wayback Machine — Candidate Figures

- **Karl Popper.** Directly connected through falsifiability and the idea that empirical claims must be vulnerable to refutation. Famous, male, European; useful but not diverse. Prompt: Ask Popper why a smooth paragraph cannot rescue an unfalsifiable hypothesis.
- **Robert K. Merton.** Connected through organized skepticism and science as communal validation. Famous in sociology but less known to undergraduates. Prompt: Ask Merton how communal scrutiny should change a student's first research question.
- **Dorothy Maud Wrinch.** Worked at the boundary of scientific theorizing and model error; useful for showing that elegant theories need testable constraints. Lesser-known woman scientist, Wikipedia-accessible. Prompt: Ask Wrinch what makes a model beautiful but still wrong.

Diversity note: candidates for this chapter may skew toward twentieth-century Euro-American philosophy, statistics, and scientific-writing traditions unless the later selection pass deliberately balances gender, geography, discipline, and era across the full book.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Novices often confuse topic, question, and hypothesis because all three can be expressed as noun phrases. Worked comparisons and forced one-sentence claims help; failure modes include letting literature-review breadth masquerade as a research problem.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 7. Representation and Display Research

A three-column comparison display is useful: Topic / Research Question / Hypothesis. Example: "AI tutoring" / "How does feedback specificity affect retention in programming practice?" / "Socratic AI feedback improves two-week unassisted retention more than direct-answer feedback."

## 9. Sourcing Notes

Primary sources are strongest for methodology, statistics, causal inference, visualization, and genre theory. Some practical guidance sources are textbooks, reporting guidelines, or editorial policies rather than empirical studies; use them as standards and pedagogical scaffolds, not as evidence that a learning intervention works. Verify exact journal AI-disclosure language, reporting checklists, and software recommendations immediately before drafting.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI as an interrogation partner for a vague observation: ask what would have to be measured, what rival hypotheses could explain the same observation, and what would make the idea falsifiable.
- Use AI to produce a menu of possible research-question framings, then require the student to choose, reject, and justify one based on their actual domain, data, and literature gap.
- Use AI to identify search terms, adjacent fields, and possible foundational researchers, treating every citation or title as an unverified lead.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not ask AI to generate the hypothesis the paper will own. That delegates the central human act of problem formulation.
- Do not let AI-written prose create momentum before the claim can be stated in one sentence.
- Do not accept an AI statement that an idea is important or novel without source verification and domain judgment.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI helped pressure-test the topic, the disclosure should say it was used for hypothesis interrogation or search-term generation, and should name what the author decided without AI: the final research question, scope, and falsification condition.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Here is my observation and what data I can access. List five ways this could become a testable research question, then for each one state what evidence would make it false. Do not choose for me.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
