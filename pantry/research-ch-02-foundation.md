## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Appelbaum et al., _Journal Article Reporting Standards for Quantitative Research in Psychology_ (2018, American Psychologist).** Gives concrete reporting expectations for design, variables, sampling, measures, and analysis. This chapter can use JARS as the bridge between a hypothesis and the evidence plan.
- **Schulz, Altman, and Moher for the CONSORT Group, _CONSORT 2010 Statement_ (2010, BMJ / PLoS Medicine).** A primary reporting guideline for randomized trials; useful for showing that source/design evaluation is structured, not impressionistic.
- **von Elm et al., _The STROBE Statement_ (2007, PLoS Medicine).** Provides a comparable reporting checklist for observational studies. It anchors the chapter's distinction between what different designs can and cannot support.
- **Nosek et al., _The preregistration revolution_ (2018, PNAS).** Supports the chapter's "lock the hypothesis" gate and shows how preregistration reduces later ambiguity about what was planned.

### Key empirical cases
- **CONSORT flow diagrams in clinical trials.** These diagrams document allocation, attrition, and analysis populations; good for showing that design quality is visible before prose polish.
- **STROBE-compliant cohort study reports.** Use a cohort paper with explicit participants, variables, bias, and statistical methods as a source-evaluation exercise.
- **Illustrative AI-tutoring design comparison.** Compare RCT, quasi-experiment, and observational platform-log study for the same hypothesis. Label as illustrative.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Design determines the strength of the claim. Reporting standards agree that readers need enough detail about participants, variables, measurement, bias, and analysis to evaluate whether the design tests the hypothesis.

### What is disputed
Checklists improve transparency but do not guarantee validity. A paper can be CONSORT- or STROBE-complete and still have a weak research question, poor construct validity, or unjustified interpretation.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
Transparent reporting norms have broadened into preregistration, data/code sharing, and AI-use disclosure. High-risk content: specific journal policies and software recommendations.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This chapter turns the book's schema principle into an evidence plan. Students must supply domain judgment about what counts as a fair test; AI can list design tradeoffs but cannot decide whether the design is adequate for the claim.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Students need prior knowledge of variables, comparison groups, and study designs. Common misconception: a prestigious source is automatically methodologically adequate. Teach by contrasting two papers on the same topic with different designs.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 9. Sourcing Notes

Primary sources are strongest for methodology, statistics, causal inference, visualization, and genre theory. Some practical guidance sources are textbooks, reporting guidelines, or editorial policies rather than empirical studies; use them as standards and pedagogical scaffolds, not as evidence that a learning intervention works. Verify exact journal AI-disclosure language, reporting checklists, and software recommendations immediately before drafting.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to compare candidate designs and surface tradeoffs such as RCT versus quasi-experiment versus observational study.
- Use AI to read an abstract or methods excerpt for red flags: missing control group, weak baseline, ambiguous sample, or causal language unsupported by design.
- Use AI to generate a source-evaluation matrix template with columns for claim, design, sample, measure, effect size, and limitation.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not use AI to decide whether a source is credible based only on prestige or summary. The author must inspect methods and original claims.
- Do not let AI collapse different designs into a generic evidence-supports statement.
- Do not accept AI-generated citations or source summaries without opening the source.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI helped evaluate sources, disclose it as source-audit assistance, not as literature review authorship; record which sources were human-verified.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Given this hypothesis and these three possible designs, list what each design can support, what it cannot support, and the most likely reviewer objection.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
