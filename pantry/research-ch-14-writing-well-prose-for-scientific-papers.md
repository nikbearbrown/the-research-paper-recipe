## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Joseph M. Williams and Joseph Bizup, _Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace_ (multiple editions, book).** Best source for sentence-level clarity, characters/actions, nominalizations, and cohesion.
- **Gopen and Swan, _The Science of Scientific Writing_ (1990, American Scientist).** Classic article on reader expectation, stress position, topic position, and sentence comprehension.
- **George Orwell, _Politics and the English Language_ (1946, essay).** Not scientific writing per se, but powerful on stale language and abstraction; use sparingly as rhetoric lineage.
- **Helen Sword, _Stylish Academic Writing_ (2012, book).** Empirical and practical critique of academic fog, nominalization, and lifeless prose across disciplines.

### Key empirical cases
- **Nominalization rewrite exercise.** Turn "the measurement of improvement was conducted" into actor/action prose.
- **Hedging calibration with statistics.** Given a result and design, choose demonstrate/show/suggest/is consistent with.
- **Passive voice audit in Methods vs Discussion.** Keep passive when agent is irrelevant; restore active when decisions need accountability.

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Scientific prose should transmit claims precisely, with confidence calibrated to evidence. Nominalizations, vague quantifiers, unnecessary passives, and uncalibrated hedges increase cognitive load and can change the claim.

### What is disputed
Disciplines differ in tolerance for first person, passive voice, and directness. The chapter should frame active prose as accountability, not as a universal ban on disciplinary convention.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
AI editing makes fluent prose cheap, increasing the premium on authorial judgment. The risk is not awkward style; it is polished sentences whose epistemic force no longer matches the evidence.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Students often equate scientific style with impersonality. Teach that precision, not stiffness, is the goal. Before/after contrast works better than rule memorization.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to find nominalizations, noun strings, vague quantifiers, throat-clearing, and evasive passive constructions.
- Use AI to offer contrastive rewrites that the author can inspect, reject, or adapt.
- Use AI to calibrate hedging only when given the evidence strength, design, and statistical result.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not use AI to make prose smoother before claim accuracy is checked.
- Do not accept AI rewrites that change causal force, uncertainty, population scope, or evidentiary strength.
- Do not let AI erase the author disciplinary register or accountability by making everything generic.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI substantially edited prose, disclose according to journal policy. The author should compare before/after text for claim drift, not only readability.

### Candidate classroom prompt

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
