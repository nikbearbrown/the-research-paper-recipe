## 1. Primary Sources

### Foundational papers and texts
- **Munafò et al., _A manifesto for reproducible science_ (2017, Nature Human Behaviour).** Gives a broad framework for improving credibility: methods, reporting, reproducibility, evaluation, and incentives.
- **Ioannidis, _Why Most Published Research Findings Are False_ (2005, PLoS Medicine).** Supports the need for claim audit and statistical integrity checks.
- **Open Science Collaboration, _Estimating the reproducibility of psychological science_ (2015, Science).** Empirical anchor for why published claims need verification.
- **Kerr, _HARKing_ (1998, Personality and Social Psychology Review).** Supports the audit against post-hoc claims presented as planned.

### Key empirical cases
- **Claim audit of a Discussion section.** Highlight claims and label supported, overstated, unsupported, or needs qualification.
- **Statistical reporting audit.** Check whether every p-value has test statistic, df, effect size, and CI where appropriate.
- **Naive-reader test.** Ask an uninvolved reader what the section claims and compare to author intent.

---

## 2. The Core Concept — State of the Field

### What is settled
Quality control must separate logic, evidence, statistics, and style. Clearer prose can make weak reasoning harder to detect, so claim verification must precede polish.

### What is disputed
How much reproducibility infrastructure is reasonable varies by field and resource level. Some critiques of replication metrics warn against one-size-fits-all judgment.

### What has changed recently (last 5 years)
AI editing increases the need for post-edit claim audits because fluency can mask unsupported statements. Data/code sharing norms continue to expand.

## 3. Application Domain Examples

- Run claim audit before style editing.
- Check statistical claims against output and tables.
- Ask compared-with-what for every quantitative sentence.
- Separate logic revisions from prose revisions.

## 4. The Book's Thesis Connection

This chapter is the book's final gate before peer review: every claim must survive evidence matching. The student must understand and defend every assertion, especially those AI touched.

A self-directed student must bring their own disciplinary expertise to the examples: the relevant construct, the plausible comparison, the meaningful baseline, and the consequences of overclaiming. Tool output can help expose gaps, but it cannot certify that the research question matters or that the interpretation is warranted.

## 6. Pedagogical Delivery Research

Students tend to revise for elegance before validity. Use color-coded passes: structure, evidence, statistics, prose.

Effective delivery should use worked examples, contrastive cases, and error diagnosis. The target reader needs to see not only the correct form but the plausible wrong form: the vague topic, the causal overclaim, the hidden proxy, the p-value-only result, the source-by-source literature review, or the fluent but unsupported sentence.

## 10. AI Use Research — When To Use AI / When NOT To Use AI


**Series calibration:** This book belongs to *Irreducibly Human: What AI Can and Can't Do*. Across chapters, AI should be framed as a tool for interrogation, audit, transformation, and drafting from a completed specification. It should not be framed as the source of the student's research question, causal judgment, construct definition, evidentiary standard, or accountability. Current publication guidance also matters: ICMJE's 2026 recommendations state that AI tools should not be listed as authors and that humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use. COPE and major publishers follow the same basic accountability principle.

### When to use AI
- Use AI to perform a claim audit: identify strong, causal, quantitative, and unsupported claims.
- Use AI to compare prose claims with tables, figures, and statistical output supplied by the author.
- Use AI to triage whether a weak paragraph has a style problem, logic problem, evidence problem, or scope problem.

### When NOT to use AI
- Do not use AI polish as quality control. Fluency can hide unsupported reasoning.
- Do not let AI verify facts or numbers unless the source data are supplied and the author checks the result.
- Do not accept AI assurance that a claim is supported when it has not cited the exact evidence.

### Disclosure and accountability note
If AI helped audit claims or compare sections, disclose it as review assistance where required. Maintain a list of issues accepted, rejected, and manually checked.

### Candidate classroom prompt
Audit this section. For each claim, label it Supported, Partially supported, Overstated, Unsupported, Needs citation, or Needs qualification, and quote the evidence it depends on.

### Research/policy anchors
- ICMJE Recommendations, updated January 2026: AI tools should not be authors; humans remain responsible for accuracy, attribution, permissions, plagiarism checks, and transparent AI use.
- COPE position statement on authorship and AI tools, 2023: AI tools cannot take responsibility for submitted work, so they cannot meet authorship requirements.
- Nature Portfolio AI policy family: generative AI use is policy-sensitive, developing rapidly, and must be disclosed or handled according to venue rules.
