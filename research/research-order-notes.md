# Research Notes — "The Research Order" (proposed chapter)

*Companion research for a chapter that the book is currently missing. Chapter 10 teaches the **writing order** (Methods → Results → Discussion → Introduction → Abstract → Title) and even distinguishes drafting order from reading order — but it begins at the moment data already exist. Nothing in the book maps the **research order**: the full arc from "something puzzles me" to "the data are in." This chapter would sit upstream of Chapter 10 and close the loop.*

---

## 1. The gap, stated precisely

Chapter 10's first line is *"Draft in the order that protects honesty; arrange in the order readers expect."* Its drafting order assumes "you have data and findings before you write" (its own words, §90). So the book teaches steps 6–11 of the lifecycle and is silent on steps 1–5 — the part where the project is actually won or lost. The writing order is the *back half* of one continuous sequence; this chapter supplies the front half and shows that they are the same hourglass read end to end.

A clean way to frame it for the reader: **research runs broad → narrow → broad, and you write it in the reverse of the order you read it.** The doing and the telling are mirror images, and confusing the two is the root of most of the failures the rest of the book catalogues.

---

## 2. The research order (verified and enriched)

The author's rough 11-step list is sound and matches how the process is taught. Refinements from the sources:

1. **Idea / itch** — a puzzle, an anomaly, a contradiction in existing work, a gap you notice. Ideas are cheap; the work is turning one into a question.
2. **Preliminary scan** — a *shallow, fast* search with one job: is the gap real, is it already filled, is it worth your time? Not the deep review yet.
3. **Refine the question** — the hinge of the whole process (see §3). Most first ideas are too broad, already answered, or unanswerable with reachable data.
4. **Deep literature review** — now go thorough: theoretical framework, what methods others used, what they found *and what they conspicuously did not* (the niche, in Chapter 9's CARS terms).
5. **Design methods** — choose a design that can actually answer *this* question given what the review revealed; pre-specify the primary outcome and analysis (ties to Chapter 7 on pre-specification and multiple comparisons).
6. **Collect / analyze data.**
7. **Results** — what you actually found.
8. **Discussion** — what it means, back-referenced to the literature.
9. **Introduction** — written now, because only now do you know your real contribution.
10. **Abstract** — last (a compression can't precede the thing it compresses).
11. **Title** — very last, once the abstract has crystallized the claim.

Two structural points worth making explicit to the reader, both well supported:

- **The loop is iterative, not linear.** Question formulation and literature searching are "an iterative process, and you will likely circle back many times" — revise the question, raise new questions, change direction as you read ([UTAS](https://utas.libguides.com/literaturereviews/setting-your-question); [Belmont](https://medlib.belmont.edu/LitReviews/develop)). Steps 2–4 especially are a cycle, not a baton pass. A good figure: the 11 steps drawn as a spiral that tightens, not a straight arrow.
- **It's an hourglass.** Broad idea → narrows to a single question and method → widens again in Discussion to implications and generalizability. The IMRaD paper *is* this hourglass frozen in print ([Tulane](https://libguides.tulane.edu/c.php?g=638809&p=4477274); [Rising Scholars](https://risingscholars.net/en/news/details/1111/)). This connects the research order and the writing order into one shape.

A complementary "whole process" decomposition from clinical research — Idea → Question (primary/secondary, quantitative/qualitative, causal/non-causal) → Acquiring data → Analysis (*prespecified or exploratory*) → Publication → Practice — is useful because it tags the confirmatory-vs-exploratory fork right where it happens ([Planning and Conducting Clinical Research, PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6476607/)).

## 3. The make-or-break step: refining the question

The author's strongest claim — *"refining the question is where most research actually succeeds or fails"* — is well supported and should be the spine of the chapter.

- The initial spark "usually leads to a general research question that is too ambitious or all-encompassing to be researched within the timeframe of a single grant or PhD project"; the work is refining it into something "interesting, yet feasible" ([From ideas to studies, PMC5846748](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5846748/)).
- There's a named psychological trap behind the rushed step: **attachment to early ideas.** Creators "form an immediate attachment to their early novel ideas and therefore select them for further development," which makes objective evaluation of viability hard ([Love at first insight, *OBHDP*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0749597822000528)). This is the mechanism behind "falling in love with your initial idea" — worth naming, because naming it is what lets a student catch themselves doing it.

### A tool to put in the reader's hands: FINER

The **FINER** criteria (Hulley et al., *Designing Clinical Research*) are the standard checklist for whether a question is ready to leave step 3 ([Cosmoderma](https://cosmoderma.org/finer-criteria-what-does-it-mean/); [Elsevier](https://scientific-publishing.webshop.elsevier.com/research-process/finer-research-framework/); [CU Boulder PDF](https://converge-training.colorado.edu/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/FINER-Criteria-for-Developing-Research-Questions-1.pdf)):

- **F**easible — enough subjects, expertise, time, money; manageable scope.
- **I**nteresting — the answer genuinely intrigues you and the field.
- **N**ovel — confirms, refutes, or extends; doesn't merely repeat.
- **E**thical — an IRB would approve it.
- **R**elevant — to knowledge, to practice/policy, to future research.

(For clinical/empirical questions, **PICO** — Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome — is the companion tool for making the question *operational*; pairs naturally with the book's running Socratic-vs-direct-feedback example.)

The pedagogical move: FINER is a gate, not a formality. A question that fails any one letter sends you *back* into the loop — and that backward arrow is cheaper now than at any later step. The cost of fixing a vague question rises monotonically across the 11 steps; this is the chapter's central economic argument.

## 4. Why the order matters — the deep engine (HARKing & preregistration)

This is what elevates the chapter from a checklist to an argument, and it ties directly into the book's existing obsessions (Chapter 7's pre-specification, Chapter 10's "don't write the frame before you know the findings," Chapter 3's assertions).

**The order is not arbitrary; violating it is a named scientific sin.** When you generate a hypothesis *after* seeing the data and then present it as though it preceded the data, you've committed **HARKing** — Hypothesizing After the Results are Known, coined by Norbert Kerr in 1998 ([Kerr 1998, *PSPR*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1207/s15327957pspr0203_4); [Wikipedia summary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HARKing)). Kerr's three forms:

1. Building post hoc hypotheses from your results and reporting them as a priori.
2. Pulling hypotheses from a *post hoc* literature search and presenting them as a priori.
3. Quietly dropping a priori hypotheses the data didn't support.

The reason this is corrosive: it "misrepresents the ratio of empirically confirmed and disconfirmed a priori hypotheses" — it launders exploratory findings into confirmatory ones, which is exactly the move a p-value can't survive (Chapter 7's large-n problem and multiple-comparisons material). **Exploratory work isn't the crime; mislabeling it as confirmatory is.** Transparent post hoc hypothesizing is fine *if you say so*.

**Preregistration is the fix that hard-codes the order.** Writing the hypotheses, design, and analysis to a timestamped, unalterable record *before* seeing data "distinguishes analyses and outcomes that result from predictions from those that result from postdictions" — it draws a bright line between data-independent confirmatory research and data-contingent exploratory research ([Nosek et al., *The preregistration revolution*, PNAS](https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.1708274114); [Center for Open Science — Registered Reports](https://www.cos.io/initiatives/registered-reports)). Registered Reports go further: peer review happens *before* data collection, so a paper is accepted on the quality of its question and method, not its results — shifting incentives toward "good science" over "good results."

So the research order isn't etiquette. It is the structural guarantee that "I predicted this" and "I noticed this afterward" stay honestly separated. The whole back half of the book (statistics, QC, peer review, ethics) is downstream protection; the research order is the *upstream* protection.

## 5. How this chapter fits the book

- **Placement:** strongest as an early chapter — a natural fit right after Ch.1 "Before You Write Anything" / Ch.2 "Foundation," or as a new Ch.2.5 framing the whole sequence the rest of the book then fills in. It is the map; the existing chapters are the territory.
- **Explicit hand-offs:**
  - → Ch.9 (Literature Review): step 4 *is* the deep review; CARS (territory/niche/occupy) is how the refined question gets situated.
  - → Ch.10 (Drafting): steps 6–11 are Chapter 10's domain. End this chapter by handing the baton: "you now have findings — Chapter 10 tells you the order to write them down."
  - → Ch.7 (Statistics): step 5's pre-specification and the confirmatory/exploratory fork.
  - → Ch.3 (Scientific Method): the a priori hypothesis is the assertion; HARKing is asserting backwards.
- **Avoid duplication:** Chapter 10 already nails the reading-order-vs-writing-order distinction and the "introduction last" argument. This chapter should *reference* that, not repeat it — its job is everything before the data, plus the hourglass that unifies both halves.

## 6. Suggested chapter shape (for a `/chapter` run)

- **Cold open:** a student who spent three months collecting data to answer a question that turns out to have been answered in 2019 — or one whose question was so broad the data couldn't speak to it. Concrete, rueful, fixable. (Mirror of Ch.10's opening, which is about the *writing* order failing; this one is the *research* order failing, one layer earlier.)
- **The hourglass:** introduce broad→narrow→broad; show the research order and writing order as one shape read in opposite directions.
- **Walk the 11 steps**, but spend the chapter's weight on step 3.
- **The refinement engine:** attachment to first ideas (the trap) → FINER + PICO (the gate) → the backward arrow (iteration is success, not failure) → the rising cost curve.
- **Why the order is load-bearing:** HARKing → confirmatory/exploratory → preregistration/Registered Reports as the order made tamper-proof.
- **Hand-off** to Ch.9 and Ch.10.
- **Worked example:** carry the book's running Socratic-vs-direct-feedback study through all 11 steps, showing the question narrowing from "does AI tutoring help learning?" (un-researchable) to a pre-registrable PICO question.
- **Exercises:** (a) run a candidate question through FINER and report which letter it fails; (b) take a published paper and locate where in the 11-step order it appears to have started writing; (c) spot-the-HARK in a constructed results section; (d) draft a one-paragraph preregistration for the student's own question.

---

## 7. One caveat / honest note

The 11-step order is the **empirical / hypothesis-driven** template. Qualitative, exploratory, grounded-theory, theoretical, and humanities work legitimately reorder or blur several steps (e.g., grounded theory deliberately *delays* the deep literature review; exploratory work doesn't pre-specify). Chapter 10 already makes the parallel disclaimer for the writing order; this chapter should make it for the research order — the *discipline* (separate what you predicted from what you found; refine before you commit) is universal even where the *sequence* isn't.
